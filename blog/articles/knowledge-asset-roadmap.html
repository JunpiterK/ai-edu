From tribal knowledge to a plant knowledge asset

AE AI Edu Archive Semiconductor process engineer · Air-gapped LLM / RAG / Agents
On this page

  2. A four-stage roadmap
  3. Deliverables, gates, and risks

The most valuable thing in your plant is not written down anywhere. It lives in the operator who hears a pump about to fail, the process engineer who knows which recipe drifts on humid days, and the technician who remembers the fix from three years ago. This is tribal knowledge. When those people retire, transfer, or take a day off, the plant becomes measurably worse at its job. This article lays out a staged, low-risk roadmap for turning that know-how into a durable, on-prem knowledge asset using a closed LLM and retrieval-augmented generation, without letting the data leave your network.

The undocumented moat

Two plants can buy the same tools, run the same materials, and follow the same published specs, yet still hit different yields. The gap is know-how: accumulated judgment about what actually works on your line. That gap is a genuine competitive moat, but it is fragile because it lives in people instead of systems. Documentation helps, but the richest knowledge is conditional, scattered, and busy. It hides in shift logs, ticket comments, chat threads, and the half-sentence someone adds while closing a maintenance action.

An on-prem LLM changes the economics of capture. Instead of asking people to author polished procedures, you let them keep working the way they already do: logging, annotating, closing tickets, and discussing exceptions. Then you build a retrieval layer that reads what already exists and answers questions over it. The know-how is captured in place. The hard part is not the model; it is sequencing the rollout so a manufacturing organization can absorb it without betting the line on an unproven system.

Tribal knowledge is a moat you cannot see on the balance sheet, and one you lose the day the person carrying it walks out the door.

A four-stage roadmap

Treat this as a capability that matures, not a project that ships once. Each stage earns the right to the next by clearing an explicit exit gate, a measurable bar that decides whether you proceed, iterate, or stop. Nothing touches the external internet at any stage: models, embeddings, indexes, logs, and evaluations all stay inside the air gap.

Deliverables, gates, and risks

The point of stages is to spend the least effort needed to learn whether the next, larger commitment is justified. Below is the same roadmap as a working table: what you build, the bar you must clear to advance, and the risk that most often kills that stage.

Table 1. Stage-by-stage roadmap for an on-prem knowledge asset.
Stage Deliverable Exit gate Top risk
1 · PoC Concept proof A retrieval demo over one narrow corpus, such as one tool's maintenance logs and SOPs, answering about 20 real questions with cited sources. On a fixed question set, answers are useful and grounded more often than keyword search, judged by two domain experts. Chasing a flashy demo instead of a representative one; choosing a domain with no clean source documents.
2 · Pilot Line trial A hardened tool for one line or team: access control, source freshness, a feedback button, and an answer-quality log reviewed weekly. Real users return unprompted; measured lift in time-to-answer or first-time-right; near-zero unsafe answers. No owner on the plant side; stale documents poisoning retrieval; a pilot that never ends.
3 · Scale Plant rollout Plant-wide coverage: multiple domains indexed, ingestion pipelines from MES, logs, and wikis, monitoring, and document governance. Adoption across teams, an SLA the plant relies on, and a cost/accuracy profile that survives scrutiny. Retrieval quality decays as the corpus grows; governance and permissions cannot keep up; infra becomes a bottleneck.
4 · Embed Routine ownership Capture becomes part of daily work, ownership sits with a named team, and refresh plus evaluation run on a schedule. The system improves without heroics: new know-how flows in and quality is tracked as a standing metric. Treating it as done; letting evaluation lapse; creating single-person dependency.

Why the PoC stays deliberately small

A tempting mistake is to index everything at once. Resist it. The PoC exists to answer one question as cheaply as possible: does retrieval over our messy real documents beat what people do today? Keep the corpus to a single domain you understand, write the evaluation questions before you build, and have experts grade the output. If it fails here, you have spent weeks, not quarters. The Pilot is where trust is won or lost

Moving from a demo to a tool a shift actually leans on is a step change. The model is the easy part; the work is operational. You need access control so a contractor cannot query proprietary recipes, source freshness so a superseded SOP does not resurface as an answer, and a feedback loop so wrong answers get logged and fixed instead of eroding trust in silence. The single biggest predictor of pilot survival is a named owner on the plant floor, not only in IT, who treats answer quality as their responsibility.

Scale surfaces the quiet failure modes

What works over one tool's logs can degrade badly over the whole plant. As the corpus grows, retrieval pulls in near-duplicate and contradictory documents, and the model confidently blends them. This is why document governance, clear ownership, versioning, and retirement of stale content, stops being optional at scale. A knowledge asset is only as trustworthy as the sources beneath it. Budget as much effort for keeping sources clean as for the retrieval stack itself.

Making it stick

The final stage is the one most organizations skip, and it is the one that separates a lasting asset from an abandoned pilot. Embedding means capture is woven into daily work: a technician closing a ticket is, without extra effort, feeding the system. A named team owns refresh, evaluation, and access. Evaluation becomes a standing dashboard metric, not a one-time launch checkpoint, so quiet regressions get caught. An air-gapped knowledge asset that is measured, owned, and fed keeps compounding; one declared done starts decaying the day the launch celebration ends.

None of this requires a moonshot. It requires sequencing: prove value on something small, harden it for real users, widen coverage only as governance can bear it, and then make maintenance boring and routine. Done that way, the know-how that used to leave with your best people becomes something the plant owns, on-prem, behind the air gap, and improving every quarter.
